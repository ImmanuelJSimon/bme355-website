Introduction

Glucose serves as the primary energy source for cells, with blood glucose levels typically maintained within a narrow physiological range following meals [1]. After carbohydrate consumption, glucose is absorbed into the bloodstream, triggering insulin release from pancreatic β-cells. Insulin facilitates glucose uptake in peripheral tissues and suppresses hepatic glucose production, forming a tightly regulated negative feedback system. This response is not instantaneous; a delay between insulin secretion and its metabolic effect is commonly observed and is often represented in mathematical models through an additional state variable describing insulin action [2].

Disruptions to this regulatory system can lead to prolonged elevations in blood glucose and, over time, metabolic disorders such as Type II diabetes. This condition, which accounts for the majority of diabetes cases worldwide, is associated with both impaired insulin secretion and reduced insulin sensitivity [3]. In such cases, delayed or insufficient insulin response following a meal results in elevated glucose levels that persist longer than in healthy individuals. Because these conditions arise from altered feedback dynamics between glucose and insulin, they are well-suited to investigation using mathematical modelling approaches.

Traditional clinical assessments, such as fasting glucose measurements or oral glucose tolerance tests, provide only limited snapshots of glucose regulation. In contrast, mathematical models allow glucose dynamics to be studied as a continuous process, capturing how the system responds over time to external inputs such as meals. State-space models are particularly useful in this context, as they represent physiological variables as interacting states, enabling structured and interpretable analysis of system behaviour.

The objective of this project is to model and predict blood glucose levels over time using a simplified state-space representation of glucose-insulin dynamics. The model is designed to evaluate whether physiologically realistic responses can be reproduced, assess how meal size influences glucose behaviour, examine system stability, and identify key parameters that govern glucose regulation. To achieve this, the system is represented using three core states: blood glucose concentration, insulin concentration, and insulin action, allowing the model to capture both immediate and delayed effects of insulin on glucose dynamics.
