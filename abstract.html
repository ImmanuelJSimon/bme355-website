Abstract

Abnormal regulation of blood glucose following a meal is an early indicator of metabolic dysfunction, yet commonly used clinical measures often capture only isolated snapshots of a continuously evolving process. To address this limitation, a simplified three-state model was developed to simulate glucose dynamics over time using physiologically meaningful variables: blood glucose, insulin concentration, and insulin action. Rather than explicitly modelling digestion and hormonal pathways, the model represents food intake as a glucose input, allowing it to capture the transition from pre-meal baseline to post-meal recovery in a computationally efficient and interpretable manner.

Analysis of the model demonstrated behaviour consistent with expected physiological responses, including a rise in glucose following a meal, a delayed insulin response, and a gradual return toward baseline driven by insulin action. Sensitivity and parameter analyses further revealed how key parameters influence peak glucose levels and recovery time, while simulations of varying meal sizes produced realistic changes in system response. Although simplified, the model successfully reproduces core features of glucose-insulin regulation and provides a useful foundation for future validation and extension toward modelling impaired metabolic conditions.
